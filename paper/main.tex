\begin{abstract}
Scientific information extraction in the nanobubble and nanocarrier
literature jointly stresses three failure modes that existing
benchmarks evaluate only in isolation: schema-fill hallucination when a
property is not stated, unit-normalised numerical grounding, and
verbatim evidence attribution. We introduce \textbf{NanoBubbleEval},
a benchmark that operationalises these failures as three structured
tasks over a shared evaluation pool. The benchmark is derived from a
52{,}519-abstract literature warehouse harvested from six public
scholarly sources, refined into an 8{,}006-record high-precision
nanobubble core, and packaged as a 500-record gold pool with a
50/450 dev/test split, five orthogonal evaluation slices, and a
40-record dual-annotator inter-annotator agreement (IAA) subset that
anchors construct validity. We benchmark three baseline families
spanning rule-based, encoder, and instruction-LLM approaches: a regex
floor (B1), a BioBERT-SQuAD-v2 extractive-QA model (B2), and
\texttt{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct} in zero-shot schema-constrained mode (B3).
On a 40-record gold-anchored test slice, B3 attains macro Acal-F1 of
$0.758$ versus $0.545$ for B2 and $0.493$ for B1 (a $26.5$-point gap
between the LLM and the regex floor), but its performance drops by
$33$ Acal-F1 points on review-paper records relative to
original-research records (a roughly $2{\times}$ larger drop than B1
or B2), and its answer--evidence consistency drops to $0.94$ from a
$1.00$ rule-based and encoder floor, indicating that
cite-hallucination begins where rule-based extraction ends.
The benchmark introduces
three metrics that decompose where models fail: abstention-calibrated
F1 isolates schema-fill hallucination, unit-normalised numerical match
distinguishes unit drift from value drift, and an answer-evidence
consistency rate flags cite-hallucination. We position v1.0 as a
\emph{preliminary, abstract-only} benchmark with documented
single-domain scope and dual-annotator validity anchoring; v1.1 will
extend to open-access full text, multi-expert adjudication, and
frontier-model ceilings.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Scientific information extraction in the nanobubble and nanocarrier
literature is an unusually demanding setting for modern NLP systems.
A single abstract typically characterises a particle population along
several quantitative axes simultaneously: hydrodynamic size, surface
charge (zeta potential), shelf-life stability, encapsulated payload,
loading efficiency, and release kinetics. These properties are
unit-bearing, evidence-sensitive, and frequently \emph{partially}
reported, so a useful extraction system must satisfy three constraints
at once. It must decline to answer when the abstract does not state a
property, recover the numeric value with the correct unit when the
property is stated, and cite the verbatim span of text that supports
its answer. A poor extractor fails along all three axes at once,
fabricating values where the abstract is silent, drifting between
units, and attaching plausible but unsupported evidence quotes.

Existing scientific-extraction benchmarks evaluate at most one of
these failure modes in a single resource. Span- and entity-level QA
benchmarks such as SciREX~\citep{jain2020scirex},
NLM-Chem~\citep{islamaj2021nlmchem}, BC5CDR~\citep{li2016bc5cdr},
and PubMedQA~\citep{jin2019pubmedqa} focus on mention recognition or
single-answer QA, but do not jointly test numerical grounding under
unit normalisation or first-class abstention. Schema-constrained
property-extraction benchmarks for materials and biomedical text
(Matscholar~\citep{dunn2022matscholar},
MatSci-NLP~\citep{song2023matscinlp},
MaScQA~\citep{zaki2023mascqa}; recent LLM-based scientific
extraction~\citep{polak2023extracting}) evaluate JSON-shaped outputs
but typically credit any non-empty value uniformly, leaving no way to
distinguish a fabricated emission from a genuine one.
Evidence-grounded factuality work
(SciFact~\citep{wadden2020scifact}, SciTab~\citep{lu2023scitab},
retrieval-augmented evaluation~\citep{ru2024ragchecker}) anchors
claims to retrieved evidence, but does not assess the quantitative
correctness of a claim or the precision of the span that supports it.

Three properties of the nanobubble / nanocarrier literature make it a
particularly informative stress test. First, it is
\emph{cross-disciplinary}: the same property schema must apply across
biomedical drug delivery, environmental water treatment, ultrasound
contrast imaging, and fundamental colloidal physics, each with its
own descriptive conventions. Second, it is \emph{unit-heavy}: size in
nm versus \textmu{}m, zeta potential in mV, stability in hours versus
days, and loading efficiency as percent versus fraction are all in
routine use, often within the same paper. Third, it is \emph{sparse}:
a typical abstract reports two to three of the six headline
properties and remains silent on the rest, so any benchmark that does
not credit \NR{} as a distinct correct answer systematically
overestimates extractor quality.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Motivated by these gaps, we make four contributions:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item We define \emph{three structured extraction tasks over a shared
record set} for the nanobubble and nanocarrier literature: schema
extraction, numerical grounding, and evidence attribution. The same
500-record evaluation pool is reprojected into three task views,
which enables decomposed error attribution within an example rather
than across separate benchmarks (\S\ref{sec:task}).
\item We release \textbf{NanoBubbleEval-v1.0}, a literature-derived
benchmark drawn from a 52{,}519-abstract deduplicated warehouse, an
8{,}006-record high-precision nanobubble core, and a 500-record
benchmark pool with a 50/450 dev/test split, five orthogonal
evaluation slices, and a 40-record dual-annotator IAA subset
(\S\ref{sec:construction}, \S\ref{sec:release}).
\item We propose \emph{abstention-calibrated F1} as the headline
metric for schema extraction. By treating \NR{} as a first-class
correct answer rather than a missing value, the metric isolates
schema-fill hallucination from genuine extraction skill and produces
a baseline-vs-naive gap that is reportable on every slice
(\S\ref{sec:metrics}).
\item We benchmark three baseline families spanning rule-based,
encoder, and instruction-LLM approaches (regex; PubMedBERT QA-span;
\texttt{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct} in zero-shot schema-constrained mode).
On a 40-record gold-anchored test slice, the LLM baseline reaches
macro Acal-F1 of $0.758$ versus $0.493$ for the regex floor, with
the largest per-field gap on \texttt{size} (\,$+0.12$ Acal-F1, B3
over B1) and the largest absolute drop on review-paper records
(\,$-0.33$ Acal-F1, original $\rightarrow$ review for B3).
Inter-annotator agreement on the same 40-record subset is
\TBD{insert macro $\kappa$ once the second-annotator pass is
finalised}, anchoring construct validity (\S\ref{sec:validity}).
\end{itemize}

We position v1.0 as a \textbf{preliminary, abstract-only} benchmark
with declared single-domain scope and dual-annotator validity
anchoring. v1.1 will extend the gold pool to open-access full-text
records, add a second expert annotator for multi-rater adjudication,
and report frontier-model ceilings (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Nanobubble research has evolved along two partly distinct directions. One line of work focuses on nanobubbles as interfacial or bulk physicochemical entities whose persistence challenges classical dissolution theory. Another line emphasizes their use in applications such as water treatment, agriculture, mineral processing, membranes, imaging, and therapy. Despite rapid growth, findings are typically reported in heterogeneous formats, making it difficult to use this literature for systematic machine learning tasks. This gap motivates the development of NB-LitBench. Scientific information extraction benchmarks fall into three broad
families, each capturing a different facet of the problem.

\paragraph{Span and entity QA.}
SciREX~\citep{jain2020scirex}, BC5CDR~\citep{li2016bc5cdr},
NLM-Chem~\citep{islamaj2021nlmchem}, and
PubMedQA~\citep{jin2019pubmedqa} evaluate mention recognition or
single-answer QA on biomedical and scientific text. These resources
are well-suited to entity-level NLP, but they do not test joint
numerical grounding or treat abstention as a first-class output, so a
model that fabricates plausible spans and a model that cites
unsupported evidence are scored equivalently.

\paragraph{Schema-constrained property extraction.}
Matscholar~\citep{dunn2022matscholar},
MatSci-NLP~\citep{song2023matscinlp},
MaScQA~\citep{zaki2023mascqa}, and recent
LLM-based property extraction~\citep{polak2023extracting} evaluate
JSON-shaped or table-shaped outputs against a fixed schema. They
share NanoBubbleEval's structured-output framing but typically credit
any non-empty value uniformly, with no metric that penalises emission
where the source is silent.

\paragraph{Evidence-grounded factuality.}
SciFact~\citep{wadden2020scifact}, SciTab~\citep{lu2023scitab}, and
recent retrieval-augmented evaluation
frameworks~\citep{ru2024ragchecker} score whether a claim is
\emph{supported} by retrieved evidence. These resources address one
of our three failure modes (evidence attribution) but do not assess
the quantitative correctness of a claim under unit normalisation, and
they do not catch the case where the answer is correct but the cited
span does not contain it.

\paragraph{How NanoBubbleEval differs.}
NanoBubbleEval departs from this prior work along four axes. First,
three structured tasks share a single record set, which lets us
decompose failure modes \emph{within} an example rather than compare
performance across heterogeneous benchmarks. Second, an explicit
\NR{} convention is scored under abstention-calibrated F1, which
distinguishes a correct abstain from an incorrect emission. Third,
unit-normalised numerical match isolates unit drift from value drift.
Fourth, an answer-evidence consistency rate catches
cite-hallucination, in which the answer is correct but the cited span
does not contain it, as a first-class failure rather than folding it
into a span F1. We make no claim that v1.0 matches the annotation
depth of expert-curated single-task benchmarks; instead, we position
v1.0 as a structured \emph{multi-task} benchmark anchored by
dual-annotator agreement on a stratified IAA subset, with explicitly
declared abstract-only scope.

\subsection{Foundational studies on nanobubble observation and stability}

Early work established the experimental basis for surface nanobubbles. Atomic force microscopy studies reported nanoscale gas domains on hydrophobic surfaces and examined their behavior at solid--water interfaces \citep{Lou2000NanobubblesSolid,Tyrrell2001ImagesNanobubbles}. Subsequent studies further characterized nanobubbles at hydrophobic interfaces and linked their properties to contact angle, interfacial structure, and substrate effects \citep{Zhang2008NanobubblesInterface,Borkent2007SuperstabilitySurface}. These works remain central because they frame the key physical puzzle: nanobubbles persist far longer than expected from simple Laplace-pressure-driven dissolution.

Bulk nanobubbles introduce a related but distinct challenge. Early solution-phase studies argued that their stability requires a physicochemical explanation involving gas--liquid interfaces, charge, and solution chemistry \citep{Ohgaki2009PhysicochemicalApproach}. More recent work continues to debate how bulk nanobubbles should be identified and distinguished from other nanoscale entities in solution \citep{Jadhav2020BulkNanobubbles}. Stability mechanisms remain actively studied, with recent papers highlighting gas oversaturation, surface charge, dynamic equilibrium, and electrostatic effects \citep{Tan2020BulkNanobubbles,Zhou2021GenerationStability,Tan2021StabilitySurface}. These differences are important for dataset construction: nanobubble records are not interchangeable, as surface and bulk systems differ in generation, measurement, and interpretation.

\subsection{Recent advances in generation and characterization}

Recent work has increasingly focused on controllable generation and reproducible characterization. Methods such as periodic pressure variation, ceramic membrane filtration, ultrasonication, Henry’s-law-based approaches, and electric-field-assisted generation have been used to produce nanobubbles under different conditions \citep{Wang2019GenerationStability,Ahmed2018GenerationNanobubbles,Yasuda2018GenerationReduction,Ferraro2020HenryMethod,Ghaani2020MassiveGeneration}. These studies show that generation method strongly influences size distribution, concentration, gas composition, and stability, and therefore must be treated as a first-class attribute in any structured dataset.

Characterization remains heterogeneous. Studies report size, zeta potential, concentration, and gas density using different instruments and assumptions \citep{Zhang2020ExperimentalStudy,Zhou2020UltrahighDensity,Li2021BulkNanobubbles}. Other work emphasizes electrostatic and double-layer interpretations of stability \citep{Meegoda2019ApplicationDiffused}. As a result, measurements are often not directly comparable across studies. NB-LitBench addresses this by explicitly encoding characterization method, measured property, and evidence source.

\subsection{Environmental, water, and materials applications}

Environmental and water engineering applications represent a large portion of recent nanobubble research. Reviews and application studies describe their use in water and wastewater treatment, aeration, contaminant removal, membrane processes, and ecological restoration \citep{Jia2023NanobubblesWater,Zhou2022UntappedPotential}. Mineral processing and separation applications have also been explored \citep{Azevedo2019BulkNanobubbles}. Individual studies further demonstrate effects on adsorption and membrane performance, for example in heavy-metal removal and nanobubble-assisted membrane processes \citep{Kyzas2018NanobubblesEffect}.

Agricultural and biological applications have also gained attention. Studies have examined gas-specific nanobubbles for seed germination and plant growth, including air, oxygen, nitrogen, and carbon dioxide systems \citep{Ahmed2018InfluencesOxygen}. Other work reports improvements in crop productivity and nutrient utilization \citep{Wu2019EnhancementTomato,Wang2021NanobubblesPromote}. These variations highlight the importance of application-domain labeling in a structured benchmark.

\subsection{Biomedical and translational nanobubbles}

Biomedical studies use nanobubbles in a different context. Here, they are typically engineered as stimulus-responsive carriers, ultrasound-active agents, or oxygen-delivery platforms. Reviews describe their use in targeted therapy and imaging, often in combination with ultrasound \citep{Xiong2021StimuliResponsive,Su2021CurrentAdvances}. Experimental studies have demonstrated ultrasound-triggered drug release, oxygen delivery for photodynamic therapy, and enhanced delivery via micro/nanobubble-assisted ultrasound \citep{Batchelor2020NestedNanobubbles,Song2020BiogenicNanobubbles,DuanL2020MicroNano}. These applications require additional labels beyond basic physical properties, including payload, loading efficiency, release profile, and therapeutic context.

\subsection{Need for a structured nanobubble benchmark}

Despite extensive research activity, the nanobubble literature is not benchmark-ready. Studies report different types of systems (surface, bulk, micro/nanobubbles, and engineered biomedical nanobubbles), use different generation and characterization methods, and measure different outcomes. While reviews summarize parts of the field, they do not provide a structured dataset suitable for model development or evaluation \citep{Zhou2021GenerationStability,Tan2021StabilitySurface,Jia2023NanobubblesWater,Xiong2021StimuliResponsive}.

NB-LitBench addresses this gap by converting publicly available literature into structured records with fields such as application domain, document type, nanobubble type, generation evidence, characterization evidence, and task-relevant attributes. This enables benchmark tasks including domain classification, multi-label property tagging, literature retrieval, evidence attribution, and downstream knowledge-graph construction. Rather than presenting another narrative review, this work provides a data resource that makes a fragmented experimental literature usable for machine learning evaluation.

\section{Task and Schema}
\label{sec:task}

We frame nanobubble property extraction as a multi-task structured
prediction problem over a shared schema. Given a record $r$ with
title $t_r$ and abstract $a_r$, NanoBubbleEval defines three
evaluation views over an 18-field normalised schema $\mathcal{S}$.
Six fields are designated \emph{headline} because abstract-level
coverage and dual-annotator agreement support reliable per-field
reporting; the remaining twelve are \emph{provisional} and retained
for schema stability and forward compatibility
(Table~\ref{tab:schema_scope}). Three task views are projected from
the same gold-pool annotation pass, which lets us attribute errors
to specific failure modes for the same example.

\paragraph{Task 1: structured extraction.} Given $a_r$, predict
$\hat{V}=\{\hat{v}_f : f \in \mathcal{S}\}$, where each $\hat{v}_f$
is either a normalised value or \NR{}. Scored by per-field
exact-match F1 and by abstention-calibrated F1
(\S\ref{sec:metrics}).

\paragraph{Task 2: numerical grounding.} Given $a_r$ and a target
field $f$ with a numeric type, predict
$(\hat{x}_f, \hat{u}_f)$, the numeric value and its normalised unit.
Scored by tolerance-bounded match on $\hat{x}_f$ after unit
normalisation, with unit accuracy reported as a separate axis to
disentangle unit drift from value drift.

\paragraph{Task 3: evidence attribution.} Given $a_r$, $f$, and the
predicted answer, return a verbatim span $\hat{e}_f$ of $a_r$ that
supports the answer (or \NR{}). Scored by character-set IoU against
the gold span and by an \emph{answer-evidence consistency rate}, the
fraction of records in which a correct $\hat{v}_f$ is paired with a
span that contains the value.

\paragraph{Why \NR{} is a first-class output.}
A naive scoring rule that compares emitted values to gold values
without distinguishing absence from emission penalises a model that
correctly abstains and rewards a model that fabricates a plausible
value. In abstract-derived extraction over a sparse domain like
nanobubble characterisation, where on average only $2.4$ of the six
headline fields are reported per abstract on the 40-record gold
slice, this conflation systematically overstates extractor quality. Abstention-calibrated F1
(\S\ref{sec:metrics}) credits a correct \NR{} as a true positive on a
\emph{distinct} class, separating schema-fill hallucination from
genuine extraction skill.

\section{Benchmark Construction}
\label{sec:construction}

NanoBubbleEval is built through a five-stage pipeline that produces a
regenerable warehouse, a high-precision domain core, a stratified
gold pool, and three task views. Every stage is keyed to public
scholarly APIs, and the pipeline ships with the release so the
warehouse can be reconstructed end-to-end from the raw API endpoints.

\paragraph{Warehouse harvesting.}
We harvest abstracts and metadata from PubMed (NCBI Entrez),
Europe PMC, OpenAlex, CrossRef, Semantic Scholar, and
ClinicalTrials.gov v2 across five query families: nanobubble core,
ultrasound and contrast imaging, delivery and release, biomedical
nanocarriers, and environmental and agricultural applications.
Records are harvested under checkpointed crawl stages so partial
harvests are preserved across rate-limited APIs, and we deduplicate
in strict order: DOI, then PMID/PMCID, then normalised title, then
URL. The final deduplicated warehouse contains \textbf{52{,}519
records}, 70.8\% of which carry an abstract
(Table~\ref{tab:warehouse_stats}). The source mix is dominated by
OpenAlex and PubMed, with Europe PMC, CrossRef, Semantic Scholar,
and ClinicalTrials.gov contributing the long tail.

\paragraph{Tier curation and high-precision core.}
We assign tier labels A, B, or C to every record by scoring lexical
proximity to nanobubble-core terms in title and abstract. Tier A is
further split into A1 (records with explicit nanobubble lexical
evidence) and A2 (records with adjacent ultrasound or gas-carrier
vocabulary). The \textbf{high-precision core} (8{,}006 records:
3{,}577 A1 plus 4{,}429 A2) is the source pool from which benchmark
candidates are drawn. The core preserves enough cross-domain coverage
to support the five-slice analysis below while filtering out the
broader nanoparticle long tail.

\paragraph{Gold pool and dev/test split.}
The 500-record \textbf{gold pool} is sampled from the high-precision
core, balanced across the nanobubble-vs-nanoparticle label, document
type, and field-coverage signals. We split it into a 50-record
\textbf{dev} set used for prompt and threshold tuning and a 450-record
\textbf{test} set held out for headline reporting, stratified by
\texttt{nb\_label} $\times$ \texttt{document\_type}. The 40-record
IAA subset (\S\ref{sec:validity}) is sampled from the gold pool with
matching stratification and is pinned to the test split, so the
highest-quality dual-annotator records appear where headline
reporting needs them most.

\paragraph{Task-view packaging.}
The same 500 gold records are reprojected into three task views: a
\emph{structured extraction} view that asks for a full schema fill, a
\emph{numerical grounding} view restricted to the four numeric
headline fields with their values, units, and tolerance specifications,
and an \emph{evidence attribution} view that asks for a verbatim
supporting span per emitted field. A single annotation pass
populates all three views via a deterministic projection step,
guaranteeing that errors observed across tasks reflect genuine model
failure modes rather than mismatched gold labels.

\section{Dataset Release and Artifact Organization}
\label{sec:release}

The v1.0 release is organised into a Python package, a layered data
release, and an evaluation harness. The package
(\texttt{nanobubbleval}) implements the schema, the unit normaliser,
the dev/test split builder, the inter-annotator reconciler, the
evaluator, and a baseline interface; subclasses of the baseline
abstract class plug into the same evaluation harness without
modification. The data release is structured by lifecycle stage: a
deduplicated warehouse manifest, the high-precision nanobubble core,
the gold pool with its five-slice annotations, the three task views
described above, the 40-record IAA subset with per-annotator labels,
and a Croissant 1.1 metadata file populated with the
NeurIPS-required responsible-AI fields.

We additionally release the annotation guideline used for the IAA
subset, the deterministic split files (with a leakage-audit report
verifying disjointness across splits), and the four numbered
pipeline scripts that reproduce every artifact end-to-end from a
clean checkout. A complete listing of release artifacts and their

adjudicated gold-hard subset are released alongside the gold pool to
allow downstream users to recompute agreement statistics and to apply
their own match policies.

\section{Validity Safeguards}
\label{sec:validity}

A 500-record gold pool is small relative to expert-curated single-task
benchmarks, so v1.0 supports its evaluation claims through five
explicit safeguards rather than through scale alone.

\paragraph{Schema and unit normalisation.}
Every emitted value is validated against the 18-field schema, and
every numeric field is normalised to a canonical unit (nm for size,
mV for zeta potential, hours for stability, percent for loading
efficiency) before scoring. Out-of-vocabulary unit strings are
flagged but never silently coerced, so unit drift surfaces explicitly
in the metric tables rather than being absorbed into a single
correctness score.

\paragraph{Dual-annotator IAA subset ($n=40$).}
A 40-record subset, stratified by
\texttt{nb\_label} $\times$ \texttt{document\_type}, is independently
annotated by two annotators on the six headline fields under blind
conditions. We report Cohen's $\kappa$ on the
\NR{}-versus-emit decision per field, character-set span IoU on
emit-emit pairs, and unit-normalised exact-match agreement on
numeric fields. Headline IAA results
(Table~\ref{tab:iaa_summary}, \S\ref{sec:iaa_eval}):
$\kappa=$\,\TBD{insert macro-$\kappa$ once adjudicated subset is
finalised}, span IoU $=$\,\TBD{insert macro span IoU},
unit-normalised exact-match $=$\,\TBD{insert numeric agreement}.

\paragraph{Adjudication trail.}
Disagreements on the IAA subset are adjudicated by the first author
under a logged decision rule. The adjudicated subset is the
\emph{gold-hard} reporting tier; the remaining gold-pool records are
single-annotator and are reported as a \emph{gold-lite} robustness
tier with that distinction surfaced explicitly in every results
table. We do not claim multi-expert adjudication for v1.0.

\paragraph{Abstract-only declaration.}
v1.0 evaluates exclusively on abstracts. We declare this as a scope
constraint rather than a goal: a property stated only in tables,
methods, or supplementary material is out of scope for v1.0, and
\NR{} is the correct answer under our scoring rule even when the
underlying paper reports the value. The numerical grounding task in
particular is bounded by abstract-level coverage; v1.1 will lift this
ceiling by adding an open-access full-text gold subset.

\paragraph{Reproducible split.}
The dev/test split is constructed deterministically with a fixed
seed. We ship the split file, the slice-membership map, and a
leakage-audit report that verifies disjointness across splits and
confirms that the IAA subset is contained within the test split.

\section{Method and Baselines}
\label{sec:method}

We benchmark three baseline families that span rule-based, encoder,
and instruction-LLM approaches. All three consume the abstract and
emit predictions for the six headline fields under a single prompt
or pattern set; no baseline is given access to the document's full
text. Together they bound a wide range of contemporary extraction
strategies and reveal where each strategy fails.

\paragraph{B1: Regex baseline.}
Hand-written regular expressions for each numeric headline field
(\texttt{size}, \texttt{zeta\_potential}, \texttt{stability},
\texttt{loading\_efficiency}). Patterns match number-unit pairs in
the immediate textual neighbourhood of field-indicative anchor
phrases, with negative lookarounds that reject ranges and
uncertainty markers. The baseline returns \NR{} when no pattern
matches. The two non-numeric fields (\texttt{payload} and
\texttt{release\_profile}) are returned as \NR{} by B1, since regex
extraction over abstracts is too noisy for free-text fields.

\paragraph{B2: BioBERT QA-span (SQuAD-v2).}
\texttt{ktrapeznikov/biobert\_v1.1\_pubmed\_squad\_v2} -- a
BioBERT-v1.1 encoder fine-tuned on SQuAD-v2 -- is prompted with one
natural-language question per headline field (``What is the particle
or bubble size in nanometres?'', ``What is the zeta potential in
millivolts?'', and so on). The SQuAD-v2 fine-tune supplies a
first-class \emph{unanswerable} prediction head, which we map directly
to \NR{}; in addition, spans whose softmax-scaled confidence falls
below a calibrated threshold (default $0.20$) are also mapped to
\NR{}. The threshold is tuned on the dev split, and test reporting
uses the frozen value. We report B2 with this off-the-shelf checkpoint
because v1.0 is intended to expose where pretrained biomedical
extractive-QA architectures fail without task-specific fine-tuning;
v1.1 will add a fine-tuned span head on the gold-pool training split.

\paragraph{B3: \texttt{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct} (zero-shot, schema-constrained).}
\texttt{Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct} is prompted with the 18-field
schema, the \NR{} convention, and a single in-context worked
example, and is asked to return a JSON object for each abstract.
Out-of-schema fields are repaired or nulled before scoring, and
the same prompt produces all three task views in one pass.
Inference uses the HuggingFace Transformers runtime on a single GPU
with greedy decoding. We choose Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as the v1.0 LLM
baseline because it is open-weights, runs on a single 16~GB GPU at
greedy decoding, and is widely studied, which makes the result
reproducible by reviewers.

A retrieval-augmented baseline (RAG over open-access full-text
records) and a same-family-larger ceiling
(\texttt{Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct}) are scoped out of v1.0 because v1.0
is abstract-only; both are scheduled for v1.1.

\section{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

The three task views call for three families of metrics. Each is
chosen to expose a specific failure mode rather than to summarise
overall correctness.

\paragraph{Field-F1 and abstention-calibrated F1.}
For each headline field $f$, let $y_f \in V_f \cup \{\NR{}\}$ be
the gold value and $\hat{y}_f$ the prediction. \emph{Naive field-F1}
computes per-class F1 over $V_f$ alone, treating both correct and
incorrect \NR{} predictions as out-of-class. \emph{Abstention-calibrated
F1} (Acal-F1) treats \NR{} as a distinct class and computes F1 over
$V_f \cup \{\NR{}\}$. The Acal-F1 minus Naive-F1 gap is the
quantitative signature of schema-fill hallucination: a model that
fabricates values when the abstract is silent has high Naive-F1 and
low Acal-F1, whereas a model that abstains correctly has the two
metrics close together.

\paragraph{Numerical match (tolerance-bounded).}
For numeric fields, $\hat{x}_f$ is judged correct if and only if
$|\hat{x}_f - x_f| \le \tau \cdot |x_f|$ after both values are
mapped to canonical units, where $\tau = 0.05$ (a relative
tolerance). We additionally report \emph{unit accuracy}
$\mathbf{1}[\hat{u}_f = u_f]$ as a separate axis to isolate unit
drift from value drift.

\paragraph{Span IoU and answer-evidence consistency.}
Evidence spans are scored by character-set IoU against the gold
span. We additionally report an \emph{answer-evidence consistency
rate}: the fraction of records in which a correct $\hat{v}_f$ is
accompanied by a $\hat{e}_f$ that contains $\hat{v}_f$ as a substring.
A model that emits the right value with a wrong span exhibits
cite-hallucination, which the IoU metric alone does not isolate.

\section{Error Taxonomy}
\label{sec:errors}

To make headline numbers actionable, we hand-inspect a sample of B3
errors and classify each into one of five recurring categories. The
taxonomy is intentionally small: each category corresponds to a
metric that can detect it programmatically, so future systems can be
diagnosed against the same axes without additional human labour.
Counts and worked examples are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:errors}; the taxonomy itself is summarised below.

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{Schema-fill hallucination.} The model emits a value
when the abstract states nothing. Detected by the
Acal-F1-minus-Naive-F1 gap.
\item \textbf{Unit drift.} The numeric value is correct but the unit
is wrong (for example, \texttt{200}\,\textmu{}m for a record that
reports \texttt{200}\,nm). Detected by the unit-accuracy column.
\item \textbf{Entity--value mis-binding.} The value is correct for
some property in the abstract but is assigned to the wrong field
(for example, binding a loading-efficiency value to release
profile).
\item \textbf{Cite-hallucination.} The value is correct but the
predicted span does not contain it. Detected by the answer-evidence
consistency rate.
\item \textbf{Topic confusion (nanobubble vs.\ nanoparticle).} The
model applies nanoparticle-typical priors (for example, assuming a
polymer matrix) on a record that describes a gas-filled nanobubble.
Cross-checked against the nanobubble-vs-nanoparticle slice gap.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Ethics}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Single-domain scope.}
v1.0 covers nanobubble and nanocarrier literature only. The schema
and metric design generalise but the lexical patterns and unit
normalisation rules do not, so headline numbers should not be
extrapolated to other materials-science or biomedical extraction
domains without re-validation.

\paragraph{Abstract-only evaluation.}
v1.0 evaluates exclusively on abstracts. Properties stated only in
tables, methods, or supplementary material are out of scope, and
\NR{} is correct under our scoring rule even when the underlying
paper reports the value. The numerical grounding task in particular
is bounded by abstract-level coverage; v1.1 will extend to an
open-access full-text gold subset to lift this ceiling.

\paragraph{Annotator constraints.}
The 40-record IAA subset is dual-annotated; the remaining 460
gold-pool records are single-annotator and reported as the
\emph{gold-lite} tier. Headline results are reported on the
adjudicated 40-record \emph{gold-hard} subset, and the gold-lite tier
is reported separately as a robustness check. We do not claim
multi-expert adjudication for v1.0.

\paragraph{Baseline scope.}
v1.0 benchmarks three open-weights or rule-based baselines and does
not evaluate proprietary frontier models. We make no comparative
claim about closed-API model behaviour on this benchmark; v1.1 will
add a same-family-larger ceiling and frontier-model comparisons.

\paragraph{Ethics and licensing.}
All warehouse records are sourced from public scholarly APIs under
their respective terms. The release ships a Croissant 1.1 metadata
file with the NeurIPS-required responsible-AI fields. The benchmark
contains no human subjects, no identifiable medical records, and no
patient-level information.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced NanoBubbleEval, a literature-derived benchmark for
schema extraction, numerical grounding, and evidence attribution in
the nanobubble and nanocarrier literature. The benchmark
operationalises three failure modes of LLM-based scientific
extraction --- schema-fill hallucination, unit drift, and
cite-hallucination --- as decomposed metrics over a shared 500-record
evaluation pool with a 40-record dual-annotator construct-validity
anchor. On a 40-record gold-anchored test slice, the schema-constrained
LLM baseline (\texttt{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct}) reaches macro Acal-F1 of
$0.758$ versus $0.545$ for a BioBERT extractive-QA floor and $0.493$
for a regex floor; all three baselines under-emit relative to the
human-annotated gold, but B3 reverses sign on the \texttt{payload}
field ($+0.28$ Naive--Acal gap), indicating that schema-fill
hallucination concentrates on free-text fields, and exhibits a
$33$-point drop in macro Acal-F1 on review-paper abstracts relative
to original-research abstracts -- roughly twice the corresponding drop
for B1 or B2. We position v1.0 as a
preliminary, abstract-only benchmark with single-domain scope and
deferred full-text grounding; v1.1 will extend to open-access full
text, multi-expert adjudication, and frontier-model ceilings.
